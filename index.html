A Man, A Dog, and an Old Blue Van.

(05/27/25) Good morning, everyone. Today Im going to share a little story from my roadtrip from Boise, Idaho, to my home city of Natchitoches (Nak-a-tish), Louisiana. Altogether, this was an amazing trip, filled with both worry and excitement. The trip itself lasted around five days and four nights. Today I will be particuallry focused on that first day and in turn, the first night. Allow me to provide a little bit of background to preface this story. My trip began on 05/27/25, and I had been in Boise, ID visiting my sister and niece for a couple of days. Visiting my sister and niece was not the only reason I wound up in Boise, however, as my trusty sidekick (Henry Dog aka Ham) and I were flying back to the lower 48 from the great state of Alaska in search of a Blue Van that had been purchased months prior. I discovered the van through a Facebook marketplace listing in February while I was teaching in the remote Alaskan village of Chefornak. I had been teaching in Chefornak for the past year and a half, so I was coming up on my second year. I thoroughly enjoyed my time in Chefornak, but by this time, I already knew that I would be moving back to Louisiana in the summer, and I would, in turn, finally need a vehicle of my own, so I had some decisions to make. Knowing that I would not want to pay off a car note, I decided on something used, which would lead me to the Blue 1995 Chevy G10 Van I now own today. Here's the deal: I bought the van while I was up in Alaska, so my ultimate plan was to fly down to Boise and pick it up. I would then drive my van from Boise back to Louisiana. That brings me back to the beginning of this story, around 4-6 pm in Boise, saying my goodbyes to my sister and niece, and getting ready to hit the road. I decided that my first stop would be at a place called Little Camas Reservoir. I would be lying if I told you that I knew much of this area, as I, without much thought, opened Google maps, found a body of water, and selected a campsite located near it. Altogether I really didn't know what to expect. Thankfully, the campgrounds were only about an hour outside of Boise, so the drive was relatively short. The weather was nice and cool, and Henry was just getting settled into the van. The terrain began to become more hilly, and the sun began to set. One back road after another would eventually lead me to Camas Reservoir Road. What I saw next took my breath away. To my right was a view straight out of a storybook, a blue reservoir situated between tall hills that honestly could have been small mountains. The sunset cast a golden light on the landscape, and as I exited my van, what felt like hundreds of birds began to swirl around me. I would soon come to find they made the camp bathroom their home. Filled with a surreal feeling, I snapped the photo above, and just a short bit later I drove my van just a bit closer to the water's edge, and that is where I found my rest for the night. Little words can explain the feelings felt as I awoke to the brisk chill of the morning air, and with little hesitation, I made myself a cup of coffee to sip as I enjoyed the beautiful view of Little Camas Reservoir from the open side door of my van. Henry, all the while, had a wonderful time trotting around the open landscape.

Art Loeb hike day three. The Thrill and Agony.

Today, I'll tell you a bit about my hike along the Art Loeb Trail. My hiking companions were Colt and Gabe. Colt, at this point, was a more experienced hiker, but experienced is not the word I would use for this group. Gabe and I were fish out of water. None of us quite understood the trials we would face over our four-day hike. The hike itself was three days. I say it was four because after our arrival at the trailhead near midnight, we decided to hike into the woods. Not our finest decision, as we found ourselves stumbling around in the dark trying to find a campsite around a mile into the trail. Needless to say, sleep was poor due to the slanted angle where we decided to set up camp. Day three was full of incline. Up, and up, and up, and up. Many times we joked that we were climbing the stairway to heaven. Some of the climb was marked with stone steps, which tended to be slippery in the heavily forested and wet environment. Other sections of the climb were marked by wooden steps. The moments that seem clearest to me are the pauses. Pretty much every pause consisted of our trio leaning over our hiking poles and gasping for air. Each break took about five minutes, and then, before we knew it, the climb continued. The mountains tried, and failed, to break our wills. Misery was abundant, but in that we could all share, so the burden itself was not too much to bear. It felt like will was indomitable. The night of day three was the greatest test of that will, if there was any to note. We arrived at the campsite with around an hour to an hour and a half before sunset. I cannot recall a time before that my feet, knees, back, and ankles had ached as badly. We were exhausted, sleep-deprived, and wet with sweat. The smell of rain was in the air, and for the past few days, it had showered consistently around the same time, so much of the same was to be expected for this night. The tents were up right before the rain started, and everyone retreated inside. Gabe was borrowing a friend's tent, and I shared Colt’s tent with him. It took under five minutes for Gabe to realize that his tent was leaking, and before we knew it, all three of us were wet inside of Colt’s two-man tent. Let me mention that we were all starving, as most would be after a day full of mountain climbing. Thankfully, we received hot dogs from another group of gentlemen earlier, as they would have to tap out and leave their own hike early due to exhaustion. They were very pleasant people, and they left us each with a beer as well. Due to the rain, our hot dogs were cooked in the tent. The smell was unpleasant to say the least, but half-wet and cold is better than soaking-wet and freezing, just as a cooked hot dog is much better than a raw one. None of us slept that night, as our group's piss break confirmed around three or four in the morning. The sun rose, and we rose with it, cold, miserable, and frankly, wet. Fire starting was a pitiful effort, to be sure, but as I lay my portable solar charger out my phone, I felt a small beam of warmth. I looked up and caught the image above, the sun beams coming through the forest, warming us ever so slightly, and beckoning us on for the last day of our hike.
